IT Security Fundamentals

What is IT Security?

IT Security The practice of protecting information-related assets consisting of information, data, and technologies. It's about keeping these critical organizational assets safe from unauthorized access, modification, or destruction.
Security The act of protecting and keeping assets safe.
Assets Items belonging to an organization that are considered valuable (hardware, software, data, people).

The CIA Model

The CIA Model emphasizes three critical attributes that must co-exist to ensure data security.

üëÅÔ∏è Confidentiality

Preventing unauthorized disclosure of information. Limiting disclosure to authorized recipients only.

"Allow what needs to be known to be seen/used only by those who need to know"

‚úì Integrity

Protecting data from unauthorized modification or deletion. Ensuring erroneous changes can be undone.

Only authorized parties can view, add, modify, and delete information

üåê Availability

Systems, networks, and authentication must work properly. Information must be available when needed.

Prevent denial of service attacks and website defacements

Description Attribute Affected
CEO's emails deleted from mailbox - no longer accessible Availability
Unable to access photo album on Google Drive Availability

Power outage causing servers out of service Availability

Beyond CIA

Authentication Uniquely identifying individuals to provide assurance of a user's identity. Ensures a person is who they claim to be.
Non-Repudiation / Accountability Ensuring relevant parties can be held accountable and cannot deny an action.
Example: Customer makes an order but cannot deny making it

Data-Information-Knowledge Hierarchy

üìä Data

Individual raw facts and figures

Example: Email address xyz@gmail.com - not very useful by itself

‚ÑπÔ∏è Information

Composition of different data put in context, meaningful to the receiver

Example: Complete student record (ID, name, age, email, GPA)

üß† Knowledge

Capability of understanding relationships and doing something useful

Example: Using course content to answer exams

Data Privacy vs Data Protection

Data Privacy

WHO can access what data

Defines who has access to data and what constitutes sensitive or critical data

Data Protection

HOW to protect (tools/methodologies)

Provides tools and policies to restrict access to data

Key Point: Users decide privacy, companies ensure protection. They work hand-in-hand to secure data.

PDPA & PDPC (Singapore)

PDPC (Personal Data Protection Commission) Singapore's regulatory authority responsible for administering and enforcing the Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA). Represents Singapore internationally on data protection issues.
PDPA (Personal Data Protection Act) Provides baseline standard of protection for personal data in Singapore. Governs collection, use, disclosure, and care of personal data. Contains 11 Data Protection Obligations.

Data Breach Notification (DBN) Obligation

Criteria for Mandatory Notification:
  • Significant Harm to affected individuals
  • Significant Scale: Involves personal data of 500+ individuals
Timelines:
  • 30 days to determine if breach is notifiable
  • Within 3 days once determined notifiable

Risk Analysis

Key Definitions

Risk The combination of the probability of an event and its consequence.
Risk Assessment/Analysis The process by which risks are identified and the impact of those risks determined.
Risk Management The process of determining: acceptable risk levels, current risk level, actions to reduce risk, and maintaining risk level.

Key Elements of Risk

Assets

Anything of value to an organization

Tangible: Hardware, software, furniture, people

Intangible: Reputation, goodwill

Vulnerabilities

A weakness or absence of safeguard

Examples: No security, OS not updated, poor locks

Threats

Something/someone that can exploit vulnerability

Examples: Hackers, fire, malware, natural disasters

Qualitative Risk Analysis

Relative Risk = Asset Value √ó Vulnerability √ó Threat

Steps:

  1. Identify Assets
  2. Assign Relative Asset Values
  3. Identify Vulnerabilities
  4. Rate Vulnerabilities
  5. Identify Threats
  6. Rate Threats
Rating Scale: Use 1 (Lowest) to 5 (Highest) for all ratings

Quantitative Risk Analysis

Asset Value (AV) The value of the asset to the organization
Example: Company laptop = $1,000
Exposure Factor (EF) Percentage of value lost due to an incident. Losing 40% = 0.4; Losing entire asset = 1.0
Single Loss Expectancy (SLE) Monetary loss if a single event occurs. Formula: SLE = AV √ó EF
Example: $1,000 √ó 1 = $1,000
Annualized Rate of Occurrence (ARO) How likely/often the event occurs in a year
Example: 7 laptops lost/stolen in a year
Annual Loss Expectancy (ALE) Formula: ALE = ARO √ó SLE
Example: 7 √ó $1,000 = $7,000

Risk Management Strategies

1. Risk Mitigation

Systematic approach to reduce exposure or likelihood

Example: Implement wireless security

2. Risk Acceptance

Willing to accept loss when risk occurs

Example: Bank reimburses fraud

3. Risk Avoidance

Not involving in activities that carry risk

Example: Don't host online

4. Risk Transference

Sharing/transferring risk to another party

Example: Outsource hosting

Access Control Models

Authentication vs Authorization

Authentication

Process of establishing one's identity to the system

Example: Login with username and password

Authorization

Permission to access resources

Example: User can open folder she owns but not others'

Three Main Access Control Models

RBAC - Role Based Access Control

Permissions assigned to roles, users assigned to roles. Access based on job function/role, not individual identity.

Example: Forum Moderator can edit posts, ban users; Regular Users can only edit own posts

DAC - Discretionary Access Control

Users control access to their own objects. It's all about ownership.

Key Concept: If you create an object, you become its owner with full control.

Common Usage: Windows, Linux, Mac OSX

MAC - Mandatory Access Control

Strictest control. Admin/policies control all access. Users cannot change access even if they created the resource.

Common Usage: Military, banks, high security environments

Comparison of Access Control Models

Aspect MAC DAC RBAC
Access Based On Security level & clearance User identity/group User role/job function
Management Centrally by Admin By Object owners Role assignments by Admin
Flexibility Least flexible Most flexible Moderate
Popular Use Military, banks Windows, Linux, Mac DBMS, Applications

AAA Framework

Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting - Framework for controlling access to resources

1. Authentication

Method of identifying a user. User enters credentials, AAA server validates against database.

2. Authorization

Determines what user can do after authentication. Enforces policies on permitted activities.

3. Accounting

Monitors resources consumed. Used for billing, trend analysis, capacity planning.

Malware (Malicious Software)

Malware Unwanted software running on a user's computer that performs malicious actions. Often exploits weaknesses or bugs in the target machine.

Types of Malware

ü¶† Virus

Malicious code that replicates by attaching to other executable code

Types: Boot Sector, Program, Macro

üêõ Worm

Standalone program that replicates independently

Key: Spreads faster than virus - doesn't need to attach

üé≠ Trojan

Program that appears useful but contains malicious code

Key: Requires human interaction to execute

üëª Rootkit

Modifies OS to avoid detection and gain escalated privileges

Key: Hardest to detect - hides itself

üïµÔ∏è Spyware

Spies on users, recording activities

Monitors: Online activities, search habits

üì¢ Adware

Presents unwanted advertisements

Forms: Pop-ups, unclosable windows

üßü Botnet

Network of infected computers (zombies) controlled remotely

Used for: Spam, DDoS attacks

üò± Scareware

Poses as legitimate software, tricks users

Tactics: Shock, anxiety, fake threats

üí£ Logic Bomb

Remains dormant until event invokes payload

Trigger: Date, action, or condition

üîí Ransomware

Restricts access, demands ransom for removal

Payment: Usually cryptocurrency

Malware Protection

Anti-Virus Software

Signature-based: Compares against virus dictionary

Heuristic: Examines behavior to detect suspicious activity

Personal Firewall

Monitors and controls traffic into/out of system

Windows Defender

Protects from spyware, real-time protection, scheduled scanning

Anti-Spam Software

Filters junk emails at corporate or host level

Malware Prevention

  • Education: Be careful installing software, download from reliable sources only
  • Disable Autorun: For removable media to prevent automatic execution
  • Security Software: Personal firewall, updated anti-virus
  • OS Updates: Get latest bug fixes to patch exploitable vulnerabilities
  • Application Updates: Update separately from OS to fix application vulnerabilities
  • IT Advisories: Stay informed about new threats

Ransomware Trends

üìä Key Statistics:
  • Ransomware remains the most prominent malware threat
  • 236.1 million ransomware attacks in first half of 2022
  • 51% of companies faced ransomware attacks
  • 26% of companies paid the ransom
  • Average ransom: $180,000 for big companies, $6,000 for small businesses
Case Study: Colonial Pipeline (May 2021)

Largest compromise of US critical infrastructure. DarkSide cybercriminal group attacked Colonial Pipeline (responsible for nearly half of oil & gas for Eastern US). Company paid 75 bitcoins ($4.4 million) ransom. Later, Department of Justice recovered 63.7 bitcoins ($2.3 million).

Social Engineering

Social Engineering The use of influence and persuasion to deceive people for the purpose of obtaining information or persuading the victim to perform some action for the hacker.
‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Social Engineering is an ILLEGAL act. You can be charged in court and may face jail term in some countries.

Social Engineering Techniques

üé≠ Pretexting

Creating and using an invented scenario to engage victim. Involves prior research or setup.

üÜî Identity Theft

Fraud using another person's identity to access resources or obtain credit.

üé£ Phishing

Obtaining sensitive information by masquerading as trusted entity in email sent to large group.

üéØ Spear Phishing

Targeting specific groups with something in common. More successful than random phishing.

üìû Vishing

Variation of phishing using voice communications. Takes advantage of trust in telephone network.

üö™ Tailgating

Unauthorized person follows someone to restricted area without consent.

üóëÔ∏è Dumpster Diving

Gathering information through careless disposal of documents or data storage devices.

üëÄ Shoulder Surfing

Looking over someone's shoulder while doing something confidential.

üé≠ Impersonation

Playing role of someone you're likely to trust. Occurs in person.

ü™§ Baiting

Laying a trap for victims, counting on curiosity or greed (e.g., infected USB drive).

Counter-Measures

Reality Check: No measures can fully protect from Social Engineering attacks. The human factor cannot be eliminated totally.

Protection by Controls:

Security Policy

Clear policies defining acceptable behavior and security procedures

Physical Security

Access controls, visitor management, secure disposal

Education & Awareness

Regular training on recognizing attacks

IT Security Infrastructure

Technical controls to support policies

Examples of Counter-Measures:

  • Activate screen savers or lock screen when away
  • Don't write passwords on papers
  • Shred confidential papers instead of throwing in dustbin
  • Don't stick passwords around workstation
  • Visitors must report at counter and exchange for visitor pass
  • Don't allow visitors to wander by themselves
  • Verify visitors' information
  • Don't disclose information through phones or emails
  • Verify email information and don't click suspicious links

Social Engineering Trends & Statistics

üìä Key Statistics:
  • 98% of cyber-attacks relied on social engineering
  • 43% of IT professionals targeted by social engineering schemes
  • 60% of new employees at high risk
  • 30% of phishing messages get opened by targeted users
  • 12% of those users click malicious attachments/links
  • Business Email Compromise (BEC) scams cost organizations $676 million in 2017
Notable Case: RSA Security (2011)

RSA Security fell prey to phishing attack that compromised SecurID systems, costing $66 million. Attack was 3-stage:

  1. Phishing email with subject "2011 Recruitment Plan" containing malicious Excel file
  2. Installing backdoor and stealing passwords
  3. Exfiltrating data to external servers

Lesson: Even security companies are vulnerable to simple social engineering!
